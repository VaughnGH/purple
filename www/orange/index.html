I'm teaching some courses

I'm going to be teaching some public Machine Learning and Big Data courses in the Bay area in March.

I'd love to have lots of people come join me. I've given some of these courses in London, and it's been quite successful and a lot of fun for all involved. (Well, people said in the reviews that they had a good time and it was worth it. I believe them.) Since I'm originally from the States, I thought it would be fun to give it a try while back home visiting.

Here's the quick summary:

  • These are evening courses, from 6 to 9 p.m. I'd be up for doing day courses if anyone's company wants to pay me to come in. Day courses aren't my priority on this trip, but I'm happy to do some if someone's interested. Email me.
  • These are paid courses. Sorry, it's fun for all involved, but it also takes a lot of time to prepare and do well. My day job is fun, but I insist that they pay me, too. If you're from the Bay area and you think I missed some cultural clue, do let me know.
  • But you can take them for free if you get your company to host the course. As long as we get enough people taking the courses that the economics works out, you can invite your co-workers, too, as long as (somewhat arbitrarily) you don't outnumber the paying folks.
  • I'm using EventBrite for fulfillment, and they let me set up referral bonuses. So I've also set up a referral bonus for people who aren't even taking the courses. Check out the "referral only" category on the registration pages and then get all your Bay area friends to come along.

What will I be teaching? Here's the list -- add "with python and scikit-learn (and maybe OpenCV)" in each case.

  • [Register] Face recognition. How it works, what are the tricks and trade-offs. When it doesn't work so well and what to do about it. We'll build one.
  • [Register] Handwriting recognition. How it works. Tricks and trade-offs. When it doesn't work so well and what to do about it. And then we'll build one.
  • [Register] Recommendation systems. How they work, what are the trade-offs. We'll build some of these, too.
  • [Register] Statistics and how to draw conclusions from data. Probability and statistics are based on some rather simple concepts, although the mathematics has a reputation of annoying. We'll talk about how to explore data sets and when it's probably meaningful (or not) to draw conclusions.
  • [Register] Neural networks. What are they, are they over-hyped? (The answer to that one is "yes and no, actually".) And then we'll build one.

The idea with all of these courses is that you're probably good at reading docs for software packages, but getting the intuition down for complex machine learning algorithms is harder. There will be some math, but not enough to scare those who don't like math. We only have an evening, not a semester, so we'll be aiming for understanding what's going on so you can use this stuff at work (or at home), not doing fundamental research and authoring papers.
